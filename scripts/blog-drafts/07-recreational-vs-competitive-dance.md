Somewhere around age 8 or 9, a lot of dance parents face a question: should our kid try out for the competition team?

It is usually framed as if the competition team is the natural next step. It is not. Recreational and competition dance suit different families, and choosing the wrong one creates real problems — burnout, financial strain, and kids who eventually quit a sport they used to love.

Here is the honest difference.

## Time commitment

**Recreational dance** is 1 to 3 classes per week. Each class is 45 to 60 minutes. Total weekly time at the studio: 1 to 3 hours.

**Competition dance** is typically 8 to 15+ hours per week of studio time during the school year. This includes technique classes (ballet, jazz, contemporary), team rehearsals for each routine, choreography sessions, conditioning, and weekend competitions during the spring.

A serious competition dancer is at the studio more than they are at any other extracurricular. Family schedules reorganize around the rehearsal calendar.

## Annual cost

**Recreational** for 1 to 2 classes per week: roughly $1,300 to $2,600 per year all-in.

**Competition team**: typically $4,000 to $8,000 per year all-in once you factor in additional tuition, choreography fees, competition entry fees ($30 to $80 per dance per competition), team costumes ($100 to $250 per routine), and travel for regional or national competitions.

Most teams attend 3 to 6 competitions per season. Nationals (if your team qualifies) adds another $500 to $2,000.

## What recreational dance builds

Foundational technique. Confidence performing for an audience (the annual concert). Friendships within a class. Strength, flexibility, and coordination. Comfort with structured group activities. A lifelong love of dance.

Recreational dancers can train for years and become genuinely skilled without ever competing. Many dancers who eventually pursue dance in college or professionally never did competition. The competition route is one path, not the only path.

## What competition dance builds

Everything recreational builds, plus: performance under pressure, deep technical refinement (judges call out small flaws), team dynamics, resilience after disappointing scores, travel experience, choreographic vocabulary across multiple styles, and (for serious dancers) preparation for collegiate or professional dance.

Competition team dancers tend to be more technically advanced than recreational dancers of the same age — but they also pay for it in time and money.

## Who recreational dance suits

- Families with multiple kids in multiple activities
- Dancers who want to enjoy dance without it becoming the dominant activity
- Families with budget constraints
- Younger dancers (under 8) — most kids should not be on competition teams before age 8 anyway
- Dancers who love performing but do not love the pressure of being judged
- Anyone who wants flexibility to add or drop classes mid-year

## Who competition dance suits

- Dancers (and parents) who are emotionally ready for the intensity
- Families who can commit to 8 to 15 hours per week at the studio
- Dancers who genuinely love competing — not just kids whose parents want them to compete
- Families with the budget for $4,000 to $8,000 per year
- Dancers serious about pursuing dance long-term (college teams, conservatory programs, professional auditions)

## The mistake we see most often

Parents push toward competition team too early because the studio recommends it or because their kid is talented. Talent is not the same as readiness.

A 9-year-old who lights up in class but cries when they get a correction is not ready for competition. A 12-year-old who genuinely loves the structure of intense training is. The age does not matter as much as the dancer's relationship with feedback and pressure.

If your dancer is not sure, keep them recreational for another year. They can audition next season. There is no rush.

## The other mistake

Parents pull dancers back from competition too late, after they have already committed financially and emotionally. If your dancer joins a team and three months in is miserable, it is okay to let them step back. Most studios have a path for that. Burnout at age 10 ends dance careers that could have lasted decades.
