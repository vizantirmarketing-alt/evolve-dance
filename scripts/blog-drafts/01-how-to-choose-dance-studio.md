Choosing a dance studio for your child sounds straightforward until you start looking. Most studios have polished websites, smiling staff photos, and five-star reviews. From the outside, they all look about the same.

The things that actually matter — how teachers were trained, what the floor is made of, whether the curriculum builds technique progressively, how the studio handles a six-year-old having a hard day — those only surface when you ask the right questions.

Here are twelve worth asking before you sign anything.

## 1. Where did the instructors train?

Dance teaching is unregulated in most states, including Nevada. Anyone can call themselves a dance teacher. That makes instructor credentials the single most important question you can ask.

Look for instructors with a BFA or BA in dance, or extensive professional performance experience. A BFA program is four years of college-level technical training, choreography, dance pedagogy, and performance. It is the closest thing to a teaching credential the field has.

## 2. What is underneath the floor?

A floor is just a floor, right? Not for a dancer.

The wrong floor causes shin splints, knee pain, ankle injuries, and stress fractures, especially in growing kids who jump and land thousands of times across a school year. The right floor absorbs impact and protects developing joints.

What to look for: a sprung subfloor (wood construction with air gaps or foam underneath, never concrete-backed) topped with marley vinyl for traction. Ask the studio specifically what is under the surface. If they do not know, that is an answer.

Every room at Evolve has raised wood subflooring topped with marley. Six rooms, no exceptions.

## 3. How many dancers are in a class?

Smaller classes mean more correction, more attention, and faster progress. A class of 25 four-year-olds with one teacher is crowd control. A class of 10 with one teacher is actual instruction.

Ask the studio for typical class sizes by age group. Recreational classes for young dancers should cap somewhere in the 12 to 15 range. Pre-professional or technique-focused classes are usually smaller.

## 4. Can parents see what is happening in class?

Some studios are completely closed off. Others have viewing windows. A few broadcast class video to the lobby so parents can watch in real time.

Live viewing matters for two reasons: parents of young dancers can confirm their child is settling in, and parents of competitive dancers can track technical progress between sessions.

Each room at Evolve has viewing windows and a TV in the lobby that broadcasts live class footage.

## 5. How does the studio handle a child who is having a hard day?

Every dancer has rough days. The question is whether the studio is equipped to handle them.

Ask: "If my six-year-old does not want to participate one day, what happens?" The right answer involves patience, redirection, and trust, not pressure or shame.

This is harder to evaluate from a website. Trust your instinct when you walk in. Watch how staff interact with the kids in the lobby.

## 6. What styles does the studio teach?

A studio focused only on competitive jazz looks very different from one offering ballet, jazz, tap, hip hop, contemporary, and acro. Neither is wrong, but they serve different families.

If you are not sure what your dancer will gravitate toward, choose a studio with range. It is easier to explore styles under one roof than to switch studios every time your child's interest shifts.

Evolve offers ballet, jazz, hip hop, contemporary, lyrical, tap, acro, and competition-team training across all of them.

## 7. Is there a clear pathway from beginner to advanced?

A serious studio has a curriculum, not just a class schedule. You should be able to ask: "What does my child learn in Ballet 1 versus Ballet 2 versus Ballet 3? When are they ready to move up?"

If the studio cannot answer that, the curriculum is improvised.

## 8. Does the studio have a competition team, and is it optional?

A competition team is not necessary for every dancer, but its presence signals that the studio takes technique seriously. It also gives serious dancers somewhere to go without switching studios.

The right question is not "do you have a competition team" but "can my child stay recreational forever, or is there pressure to compete?" The healthy answer is yes to staying recreational.

## 9. How does enrollment work, and how flexible is it?

Some studios run on a school-year calendar (August through June) with no mid-year additions. Others let dancers join any month. Some require annual contracts, others bill monthly.

Ask: "If we need to stop mid-year, what happens?" The answer tells you how the studio thinks about families.

## 10. What is the trial process?

A studio confident in its training will let your child try a class before enrolling. Most legitimate studios offer at least one free trial.

If a studio will not let you observe or try, that is worth noting.

## 11. How does the studio communicate with families?

Weekly emails? Monthly newsletters? A parent portal? Text alerts for schedule changes?

The communication style of a studio reflects how organized and family-focused it is. Disorganized communication usually means disorganized everything else.

## 12. What does the end-of-year performance look like?

Some studios call it a recital. Evolve calls it a concert.

The format matters: is it on a professional stage or in a high school auditorium? Are tickets reasonable, or do they cost more than a Broadway show? How long is the production? How are costumes handled — included in tuition, or billed separately?

Ask before you enroll. End-of-year fees can be a surprise if you do not.

## Final thought

The right studio is not the one with the flashiest website or the most aggressive marketing. It is the one where the people teaching your child are trained, the floor is built to protect them, the class size lets them be seen, and the program has the structure to grow with them.
