When parents ask what style of dance their kid should take, the easy answer is "whatever they want." That is true but not useful. Different styles build different skills, suit different personalities, and start at different ages. Picking the right one (or the right combination) matters more than people realize.

Here is what each style actually teaches and how to think about choosing.

## Ballet

The foundation of every other style. Ballet teaches turnout, posture, port de bras (arm work), pointed feet, jumping mechanics, and the discipline of repetition. Even dancers who never pursue ballet long-term benefit from a few years of it because every other style assumes ballet's vocabulary.

Best for: kids who like structure and precision. Ballet rewards focus, patience, and attention to detail.

Best age to start: 3 or 4 in a creative-movement or pre-ballet class. Real technique training around age 6 or 7.

What it builds: body awareness, alignment, strength, flexibility, musicality, mental discipline.

If your dancer wants to pursue any serious dance training later, ballet is the floor. Even competition team dancers in hip hop and jazz benefit from ongoing ballet training.

## Jazz

The Las Vegas style. Jazz is performance-forward, personality-heavy, and technically demanding. It combines ballet technique with sharper lines, isolations, and theatrical expression. Most competition-team routines lean jazz or jazz-adjacent.

Best for: kids with strong stage presence. Jazz dancers tend to be outgoing and love performing.

Best age to start: 5 or 6 once basic ballet is in place. Combo ballet-and-jazz classes are common at this age.

What it builds: performance skills, sharp technique, stamina, versatility across styles.

## Hip Hop

Freestyle, groove, and rhythm. Hip hop is less about formal technique and more about feel, attitude, and musicality. It is also the most accessible style for new dancers because there are no ballet positions to learn first.

Best for: kids who are intimidated by ballet's formality. Hip hop is high-energy, fun, and forgiving of beginners.

Best age to start: 5 or 6 for foundational classes. Serious technique work around 8 or 9.

What it builds: musicality, groove, stamina, creativity, confidence.

A note: good hip hop training is more technical than people assume. There is real choreography work, isolation training, and floor work involved at advanced levels. It is not just freestyling to a song.

## Contemporary

Modern expression through movement. Contemporary blends ballet technique with the emotional storytelling of modern dance. It is the most expressive style and often where serious teenage dancers gravitate.

Best for: dancers who feel things deeply and want movement to mean something. Contemporary is emotional, abstract, and personal.

Best age to start: 9 or 10. Younger dancers benefit from ballet and jazz first because contemporary draws from both.

What it builds: artistry, musicality, emotional range, choreographic thinking.

## Tap

Rhythm and percussion. Tap is unique because the dancer is the instrument. The shoes make the sound. It builds musicality differently from any other style and is great for kids with strong rhythm.

Best for: kids who love music and rhythm, especially kids who tap their feet during songs without realizing it.

Best age to start: 4 or 5 in beginner tap. Tap technique progresses fast in motivated kids.

What it builds: musicality, rhythm, footwork precision, coordination, joy.

## Acro

Acrobatics meets dance. Acro combines tumbling, flexibility, and dance technique. It is the closest style to gymnastics within a dance studio context.

Best for: dancers who are also athletic, flexible, and unafraid of being upside down.

Best age to start: 5 or 6 with basic flexibility and tumbling. More advanced acro tricks (aerials, walkovers, contortion) come with training.

What it builds: strength, flexibility, fearlessness, body control.

A safety note: acro should only be taught by instructors specifically trained in it. Dance teachers without acro background can cause serious injury. Ask about the instructor's certifications before signing up.

## How to choose

For most kids starting young, the answer is a combo class. Ballet plus jazz, ballet plus tap, or ballet plus hip hop. The combo lets them try multiple styles without committing to one, and it builds technical range early.

As your dancer gets older (7 to 9), let them tell you what they love. A kid who lights up in jazz and tolerates ballet will tell you that. A kid who lives for the structure of ballet will too.

For serious dancers (10 and up), most studios recommend training across multiple styles. The dancers who go furthest are usually the most versatile. A pure ballerina is rare in modern dance careers. The dancers who book work professionally are strong in ballet, jazz, contemporary, and often hip hop.
